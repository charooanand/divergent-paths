Divergent Paths

A New Perspective On Earnings Differences Between Black And White Men Since 1940

The second half of the 20th century began with progressivist hopes for racial equality - or at least a march towards it. In particular, the 1950s and '60s ushered in landmark gains for African Americans in the form of desegregation, Civil Rights and Voting Rights.
But did legislative gains translate to economic advancement? Bayer & Charles (2018) revise current understanding on the Black-White earnings gaps in the US. By looking beyond simple differences in average incomes, their analysis paints a richer picture of income inequality.

Let's explore two different ways to visualize differences in Black and White men's earnings.

The Earnings Level Gap

If we're thinking about much more White men earned compared to their Black counterparts, a natural measure, and the approach that most studies follow, is to compare the median income of a White man to that of a Black man. This is a simple and informative statistic. However, there isn't anything especially meaningful or magical about the median. The median income of a racial group is, to be formal, the income level such that 50% of that group earn less than that level. Medians, therefore, tell us about the experience of very specific people – two men who are bang in the middle of their respective racial group's income distribution. What about two men who are the top earners in their respective racial groups? That's also informative. What about two men who are at the 15th percentile in their respective racial groups? That's also informative.

One advantage of this approach is that we can more clearly see how unemployment or non-participation in the labor force (perhaps due to incarceration) differentially shapes White and Black income distributions, and therefore the measures of the earnings level gap.

The Earnings Rank Gap

An alternative way to measure differences in Black and White men's earnings is to think about where a Black man's earnings would place him within the White income distribution. For example, say the median Black man earned $30k. This income might place him at the 50th percentile among Black men but it might only place him at the 20th percentile among White men. In this example, at the $30k level, there is an earning rank difference of 50 - 20 = 30.
